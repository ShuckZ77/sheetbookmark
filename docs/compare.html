The open-source landscape — when to choose them instead

SheetBookmark isn't the right tool for everyone. Honest pointers:

floccus — true two-way sync of your bookmark tree

If what you want is your native bookmark tree mirrored perfectly across browsers — deletions and all — floccus does that and we deliberately don't. Choose floccus for tree mirroring with your own backend (Nextcloud, WebDAV, Drive, Git) and optional end-to-end encryption; choose SheetBookmark when you want the destination to be a usable, sortable, annotatable spreadsheet with zero backend setup.

Linkwarden — self-hosted, full-featured, collaborative

The closest open-source answer to Raindrop: collections, full-page archiving, annotation, teams — if you're comfortable running your own server. Choose it when you want archival copies of pages and run Docker without blinking; choose SheetBookmark when you want zero infrastructure.

Karakeep — self-hosted with AI tagging

Automation-heavy self-hosted bookmarking (rule engines, AI tagging) for people who treat their library as a serious research workflow — and are happy to host it. Different effort budget entirely.

Linkding — minimal self-hosted bookmarking

The lightweight classic: fast, simple, reliable, easiest Docker setup of the class. Choose it for a minimal personal server tool; choose SheetBookmark if "server" isn't in your vocabulary.

Also notable: Toby — a polished tab/workspace manager (closed source, cloud-based); a different job than bookmark syncing, which is why it left this matrix.

Common questions

What is a good Pocket alternative now that Pocket shut down?

Pocket was discontinued in July 2025, taking saved collections with it. SheetBookmark is a free, open-source alternative for saving pages with notes — and because everything lives in a Google Sheet in your own Drive, no shutdown can ever take your collection away. There is no service to shut down.

Is SheetBookmark a Raindrop.io alternative?

They solve the same problem with opposite philosophies. Raindrop is an excellent polished cloud service — your bookmarks live on its servers, with its apps as the interface. SheetBookmark has no server: bookmarks live in a spreadsheet you own, the interface is any browser plus Google Sheets itself, and the code is AGPL open source. Pick cloud polish, or pick ownership.

SheetBookmark vs floccus — which should I use?

Both are open source with no vendor server. floccus mirrors your native bookmark tree across browsers (two-way, deletions included) into a sync backend you configure. SheetBookmark writes an append-only, human-usable ledger — a Google Sheet with notes, per-browser tabs and formulas — with zero setup. Mirroring plumbing vs. usable ledger: pick by which you want your data to be.

Why not just use Chrome or Firefox built-in sync?

Built-in sync only syncs a browser with itself — Chrome to Chrome, Firefox to Firefox. If you live in one browser ecosystem it's great. SheetBookmark exists for everyone else: it syncs bookmarks across Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Brave and Vivaldi into one searchable spreadsheet, with notes, that you keep forever.

How do I export bookmarks to Google Sheets automatically?

That is literally what SheetBookmark does: press Ctrl+D (or click the toolbar button) and the bookmark appears as a row in your sheet within seconds — title, URL, folder, note, timestamp, and which browser saved it. No manual export step exists because there is nothing to export: the sheet is the storage.
